Application Examples

Rapid Prototyping

This group focuses on early-stage form exploration, where the main goal is to quickly evaluate overall volume, proportion, and spatial structure rather than fine-detail accuracy. LaserMorph supports fast transitions from planar design to physical 3D approximation, a recognized need in low-fidelity prototyping workflows.

Product Design Prototypes

LaserMorph optimizes early-stage prototyping by prioritizing rapid form evaluation over fine-detail precision. It significantly bypasses FDM's deposition-based bottlenecks, reducing fabrication time from hours to minutes.

Audio Player

Compact audio player enclosure for hand-sized electronic product prototyping, testing body volume and surface transitions.

Game Console

Handheld entertainment hardware prototype emphasizing massing, interaction zones, and balance between tactile contact and volumetric presence.

Knob Switch

Rotary interaction components as rapidly fabricated tactile controls, evaluating grip and rotational affordance.

Microphone

Microphone-like handheld object with distinct head-and-handle geometry for evaluating grasp and silhouette recognition.

Radio

Compact radio form factors studying front-facing interface composition and enclosure proportioning.

Speaker

Volumetric audio product prototype. A soft expanded body communicates acoustic enclosure, frontality, and product presence through silhouette, stance, and proportion.

Architectural and Urban Modeling

To overcome single-bed scalability limits—a persistent challenge in human-scale fabrication—we developed modular X–Y tiling and mortise-and-tenon joints for large-scale architectural models.

Kimbell Art Museum

Large-scale architectural modeling demonstrating planar subdivision, mortise-and-tenon assembly, and Z-axis stacking for macro-scale models.

LaserMorph City

Urban-scale modular modeling with decomposed terrain and building units for updateable planning sandboxes.

Vertical Building

Exploration of flat typographic footprints transformed into tall, legible vertical forms through controlled expansion and stacking.

Sustainable and Logistics-Oriented Uses

This group emphasizes the relationship between planar storage, volumetric deployment, and the use of biodegradable materials. These cases show how LaserMorph can be used in scenarios where compact transport and on-demand expansion are as important as the final geometry itself.

Sustainable Conformal Packaging

Custom cushioning for fragile or irregular items like strawberries and glassware. Structures transition from flat-packed sheets into shape-conforming 3D cushions upon activation.

Marathon Cooling Sponge

Event-specific 3D forms encoded into flat sheets for compact distribution. After hydration, they expand into high-surface-area geometries suitable for evaporative cooling.

Aquatic Habitat

Cellulose-based scaffolds, including artificial seagrass and sheltering arches, provide compliant and biodegradable structures for small aquatic or semi-aquatic environments.

Beach Toys

Ultra-thin flat-packed toys expand upon contact with seawater, reducing storage volume and the need for durable plastic bodies.

Interlocking Storage Box

Self-locking containers use micro mortise-and-tenon joints for glue-free assembly, supporting small-object storage and repeated reuse through thermal resetting.

Enhanced Everyday Objects

This group explores how local geometric programming can enrich familiar sponge-based objects with differentiated functions. Rather than creating entirely new artifact categories, these cases show how LaserMorph can augment everyday objects through internal structural variation. At the same time, we also explored how LaserMorph utilizes material properties for aesthetic design expression.

3D Volumetric Bath Sponge

Transcending conventional 2D-printed sponges, LaserMorph creates true 3D characters from a single flat sheet, transforming static graphics into tactile, volumetric bath products.

Dual-texture Scrubber

Double-sided engraving integrates sparse and dense tooth patterns into a single substrate, supporting both gentle and aggressive cleaning.

Ergonomic Wrist Rest

Leveraging the soft compliance of expanded cellulose, LaserMorph provides localized cushioning that conforms to user anatomy for ergonomic support.

Tactile Postcards

Flat-packed postcards encode topographic or scenic relief into mailable sheets. Upon hydration, the sheet transforms into a tactile 3D surface, combining compact distribution with embodied exploration.

Expressive Modular Graphic Prototyping

LaserMorph as a medium for visual communication and tactile expression. Graphics are unfolded, hydrated, assembled, and touched, introducing time, surprise, and participation into flat communication surfaces.

Sustainable Sensors and Tangible Interaction Interfaces

This group examines how LaserMorph can utilize natural energy for transformation, support tactile engagement, and physically interactive artifacts. These cases suggest that the method is not only useful for fabrication, but also for shaping temporal and embodied forms of interaction.

Rain-triggered Natural Energy Sensor

Signage such as "Slippery Surface" remains flat under normal conditions and becomes more spatially prominent after rainwater activation—a zero-power strategy for context-aware public alerts.

Translucent Lampshade

This case leverages the light-diffusing properties of the expanded cellulose network. By modulating thickness via grayscale engraving, patterns emerge as nuanced light gradients upon expansion.

Multimodal Haptic Instruments

A "Pentatonic Squeeze" interface maps laser-encoded stiffness differences to musical tonalities, enabling tangible multimodal feedback through pressing and deformation.

Customizable Game Peripherals

A bio-based controller concept uses compliant material behavior and modular laser patterning to support ergonomic customization and component swapping.
