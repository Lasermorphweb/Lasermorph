Morphing

Designers can control the lifecycle of the material through four triggering factors: Water Absorption, Sealing, Dehydration, and Compression. These four factors are essential in manipulating the material's physical state, enabling flexibility, reusability, and dynamic interaction across various design cycles.

Material Morphing States

1. Water Absorption (Potential State → Activated State)

Water absorption is the first and most immediate process that triggers the material's transformation. Upon immersion in water, the material absorbs moisture, causing an almost instant expansion, up to 10 times in vertical height. The material transitions from a flat, compressed state to a flexible, elastic form, displaying the 3D shape that was pre-programmed into its structure.

  • Immerse the compressed material in water.
  • The material will expand vertically by up to 10 times its original height.
  • As it absorbs water, it becomes soft and elastic, exhibiting the designed 3D geometry.

2. Sealing (Activated State → Maintained State)

Sealing is a method to maintain the activated, flexible state of the material for an extended period. By using a plastic film, water evaporation is suppressed, allowing the material to stay in its activated state for several hours or even days.

  • After activation, cover the material with a plastic film.
  • The film prevents water from evaporating, keeping the material soft and malleable.
  • The material remains in this state for several hours or even days, supporting ongoing manipulation.

3. Dehydration (Activated State → Locked State)

Dehydration involves the evaporation of water from the material, causing the fibers to reconnect and the material to harden into a solid, self-supporting structure. This process locks the material into the shape it acquired during the activation phase.

  • Allow the material to dry naturally or accelerate the process with forced air.
  • As the material dries, it hardens, locking in the 3D shape.
  • The material becomes self-supporting and resistant to further deformation.

4. Compression (Locked State → Potential State)

Compression is a reversible process that allows the material to return to its original flat state. By heating the material and applying pressure, the material is compressed back into a 2D form.

  • Ensure the material is fully dried and in the locked state.
  • Apply heat (around 120°C) to the material.
  • Use a press to apply pressure for approximately 25 seconds, returning the material to its flat state.
  • Allow the material to cool and lock in its compressed form, ready for storage or future use.
