Kimbell Art Museum

This case examines the large-scale architectural modeling capability of lasermorph through a replica of the Kimbell Art Museum. The project uses planar subdivision, mortise-and-tenon assembly, and Z-axis stacking to build a macro-scale model from processable modules, allowing the system to handle large geometry without relying on a single oversized fabrication volume. Beyond simple model making, the case demonstrates how architectural elements can be decomposed into fabrication-friendly units while still preserving the spatial rhythm, roof logic, and overall silhouette of the original building.

The value of the case lies in demonstrating that the system is not limited to small consumer products. It can also support fast iteration for architecture-scale volumetric studies where fabrication speed, transportability, assembly efficiency, and repeatable module logic are critical. In practical terms, this means that lasermorph can be used not only for presentation models, but also for early architectural reasoning, educational reconstruction, and exhibition-scale prototyping where making, moving, and reassembling large objects are all part of the design process.
