Application Examples

Rapid Prototyping

lasermorph supports rapid product and spatial prototyping by converting flat laser-coded sheets into volumetric forms with short fabrication cycles, lightweight transportation, and low storage burden. In desktop-scale studies, it substantially reduces production time compared with FDM while still maintaining robustness under compression, handling, and repeated hydration. In large-scale studies, modular tiling, mortise-and-tenon assembly, and vertical stacking extend fabrication beyond the size limits of a single machine, making the workflow suitable for both hand-sized products and macro-scale spatial models. The key contribution of this category is that it treats expansion not as a secondary material side effect, but as a controllable design resource that links 2D coding directly to 3D manifestation.

Sustainable and Logistics-Oriented Uses

lasermorph combines volumetric transformation with biodegradability, transport compression, and low-burden deployment. The value of this category lies not only in the generated form, but in the complete lifecycle of the object: flat-packed distribution, low-energy activation, lightweight handling, and reduced environmental burden after use. Because the material can be carried compactly and activated when needed, this category is especially relevant for outdoor events, ecological installations, disposable-yet-degradable products, and mobile systems with strict logistical constraints.

Aquatic Habitat

Biodegradable micro-habitats for aquatic species such as hermit crabs, creating environments that regulate humidity and provide concealment.

Enhanced Everyday Objects

Everyday-object augmentations adding protection, reversibility, and material intelligence including bath sponges, scrubbers, and packaging.

Expressive Modular Graphic Prototyping

lasermorph is used here as a medium for visual communication and tactile expression rather than only as a functional form-making process. These studies examine how transformable relief, modular assembly, and spatialized graphics can enrich posters, souvenirs, and symbolic objects. The resulting artifacts exist between graphic design, product prototyping, and exhibition media, making them especially useful in scenarios where an image is expected to generate attention through transformation rather than through static display alone. What unifies this category is the shift from static representation to activated representation. Graphics are no longer only looked at; they are unfolded, hydrated, assembled, and touched. This introduces time, surprise, and participation into what would otherwise remain a flat communication surface.

Tactile Postcard

Dimensional reduction of 3D souvenirs into flat, mailable artifacts that reveal topographic relief after hydration.

Wrist Rest

Desktop ergonomic prototype for evaluating wrist support, height transition, and front-edge slope through continuous curvature.

Tangible Interaction Interfaces

lasermorph is used here as an interaction substrate rather than only a fabrication medium. The cases in this section demonstrate how softness, expansion, engraved stiffness, and environmental responsiveness can directly shape use, perception, and feedback. Instead of relying only on rigid electronics-first logic, these interfaces build meaning through material behavior itself, allowing form, touch, and context to participate in the interaction loop. This section shows that interaction can be distributed across geometry, material, and environmental trigger rather than concentrated only in sensors or screens.

Lampshade

Multimodal Haptic Instruments

Musical structure translated into tactile interaction using laser-coded stiffness, creating closed loops between touch, sound, and image.

Rain-Triggered Urban Interfaces

Context-aware public signage that remains visually quiet in dry conditions and becomes legible only when activated by rain or water.
